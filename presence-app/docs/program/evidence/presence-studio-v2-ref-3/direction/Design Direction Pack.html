A grammar for rooms,
not a kit of pages.

Presence is a system for creating public digital rooms — expressive, atmospheric, spatial places people can enter. This pack defines the system that holds it: the layers, the room-world atlas, the object vocabulary, the motion grammar, the studio cockpit, the mobile model, and the rules that keep it from flattening.

i.

Where Presence sits

§ 01 · position

Presence sits between four worlds. A design choice that serves only one is probably wrong; good Presence design serves at least two.

i. business website

The useful shop

Booking, pricing, credibility, search, commerce. Presence is genuinely useful — it can take an enquiry, take a deposit, hold a calendar.

bookingpricingcredibilitysearch
ii. myspace

Identity, authored

Self-expression that breaks the template. A presence that is unmistakably the person whose room it is. The freedom to be weird in public.

authorshipweirdnessauravoice
iii. tumblr

Known by taste

A moodboard graph — be known by what you collect, who you reference, what you'd be seen near, not by what you post.

moodboardinfluencesignalarchive
iv. a physical room

Somewhere spatial

A booth, an archive, a desk, an altar, a wall, a doorway. The visitor walks in. The room holds them. They leave a trace.

thresholdchamberobjecttrace
ii.

Threshold → Chamber → Object → Action

§ 02 · model

The whole product is this arc, repeated. Every Presence design — owner side and visitor side — should map cleanly to one of these four stages.

i. threshold

Desire to enter

The entry state. Cinematic, atmospheric, alive before interaction. The visitor decides whether to cross.

ii. chamber

The room itself

Where objects live. Story, work, offers, proof, archive, sound, contact. Mood shifts by world. Mobile-safe.

iii. object

Focus & aura

A single thing held in attention — an artwork, a mix, a piece, a document. Carries identity, proof, action.

iv. action

A trace, a portal

An enquiry, a booking, a save, a guestbook entry, a step into another room. Never a checkout flow.

Vocabulary discipline. The system uses these words and only these words for the public surface: room, threshold, chamber, object, surface, skin, portal, trace, moodboard, signal, archive, offering, proof, mode. Banned in user-facing copy: page, section, block, widget, template, dashboard, profile. Engineers may use whatever internal vocabulary is needed; the user never sees it.

iii.

The three layers

§ 03 · architecture

Calm shell. Wild rooms. Living traces. If the system collapses into one look, it has failed.

layer a · studio cockpit

The calm shell

The room-direction tool. One visual language, always. Dark studio chrome, hairline borders, slow motion. The shell follows the room's accent — it never expresses personality. Mobile-first: bottom-sheet inspector, thumb-zone action bar, save FAB.

Rule: The cockpit frames personality. It does not compete with it.
layer b · public rooms

The wild space

Many visual languages by design. Each room is a kit (spatial logic) plus a skin (atmosphere). Switching kits changes how space behaves, not just what colour it is. Gallery floats. Archive rules and documents. Booth pulses. Altar breathes.

Rule: Each kit is a spatial logic, not a palette.
layer c · social traces

The evidence

Footfall, guestbook, seed, attended, signed, signal, portal, corridor. Traces are not notifications — they are spatial marks discovered inside a room. A sticker on a wall. A name in a guestbook. A doorway to another room. They make the room feel alive before the network exists.

Rule: Traces are evidence, never push notifications.
iv.

Room-world atlas

§ 04 · ten archetypes

Each world implies different radius, density, motion, type weight, object shapes, and background material. None of them are a "theme." Each is a way space behaves.

i · gallery wall

Private exhibition

A spotlight. Frameless walls. Quiet editorial. The room recedes so the work can speak. Mobile: one work at a time, full-bleed, swipe across the wall.

spotlight · recede · hush · focus · drift
ii · dj booth

The booth

Crate, signal, waveform, vinyl. Marquee at the door; pinned zine board on the wall. Loud, rhythmic, but restrained — strobe used once, never as wallpaper.

pulse · scan · marquee · spin · strobe-restraint
iii · healing altar

A holding place

Round cards, soft chamber, sage atmosphere. The room breathes. Booking is calm, never insistent. New-age without astrology-app cliché — no zodiac, no crystals, no purple.

breath · glow · soft · still · warm
iv · archive / evidence

The record

Cultural orgs, oral history, grant-facing proof. Ledger left rule, archive prefixes, document treatment. Consent and sensitivity are visible in the visual language.

reveal · evidence · drawer · paper · memory
v · market stall

A shop card

Makers, stalls, independent businesses. Printed card and hand-kept ledger. Honest, tactile, unhurried — the work on the bench ticks toward delivery; pieces carry status at a glance.

stack · sort · touch · table · offer
vi · clothing label

A drop

Label, garment, cultural signal, collection. The room is a lookbook plus a stockroom plus a story. Drops arrive on the wall; sold-out states are part of the aesthetic.

drop · texture · runway · sold-out · signal
vii · consultant desk

A desk

Sharp office. Paper, margin notes, framework, proof. Monochrome hairlines, an inverted invitation, authority through precision. For practitioners selling clarity.

paper · margin · framework · proof · invitation
viii · workshop

A bench

Material, tool marks, before/after, process. The room shows work in progress; the visitor can see the maker's hand. Status is a chip, not a SKU.

material · process · before-after · tool-mark
ix · shrine / ritual

A flame

For community programmes, memorials, sacred archives. A single light. A name. A consent layer. Used sparingly. If your design system can't hold this, it can't hold real people.

vigil · flame · name · stillness · consent
x · corridor / path

A walk

Several rooms arranged into a path. Festival lineups, tours, programmes, group shows. The visitor walks one room into the next without leaving the system.

step · doorway · sequence · drift

The atlas is open. New worlds are added by writing a new kit — its spatial logic, type stack, motion verbs, object shapes, and room tokens. A kit is never just a recolour. The bbb.vision pilot is a "Gallery wall · photographer" kit pushed to its ceiling — the same kit could host a printmaker, a film stills photographer, a tattoo artist, each as a distinct skin within the same spatial behaviour.

v.

Object vocabulary

§ 05 · fifteen specimens

Objects are the nouns of the room. Each one should feel like something on a wall, on a desk, in a drawer — not like a web card with a border-radius.

i · artwork

Framed work

Image inside a held frame. Caption sits below; spotlight on hover. The room recedes to let it speak.

ii · image

A still

Frameless image with grain. Used for portraits, mood images, in-situ photographs. Sits inside an object container, not a card.

iii · text / story

A passage

Editorial body; one italic emphasis word; copper rule on the left margin. Never centred unless ritual context demands it.

iv · proof / document

A page

Press clipping, certificate, grant record. Paper-stock, a stamp, an archive number. Carries weight.

06Jun · 21:00Berghain
v · event

A date

Date as the largest type. Venue and time below. Status chip (announced · sold-out · attended).

vi · mix / audio

A signal

Waveform across the object's width. Number, title, BPM, length in mono. Plays inline; never autoplay.

vii · product / drop

A piece

A still of the thing. Price in display italic. Status dot (available · on the bench · spoken for · sold).

Enquire
viii · booking / contact

An invitation

A line, a step, a calendar — never a "lead-gen form." Single-question first. Calm primary, never urgent.

"The room is a quiet place to be still."
ix · testimonial

A witness

Pull-quote in display italic. Author in mono. Credit line, not a star rating. Never carousel.

x · moodboard / influence

A moodboard

Four to nine tiles, asymmetric, slightly torn. References, influences, what you'd be seen near. Identity by adjacency.

xi · portal / link

A doorway

Not a link — a passage to another room. Aperture mark, soft glow, the receiving room's accent leaking through.

xii · trace / guestbook

A mark

A handwritten line, an initial, a date in mono. Pinned to the wall. Visible to all visitors; moderated by the owner.

Cross the threshold →
xiii · cta

A step

Two words in mono uppercase, slight tracking. Sharp corners. Hover is a quiet swap, not a colour explosion.

xiv · archive item

A record

Catalogue number, date, brief. Hairline rules. Reads as a ledger row, not a card. Ages well.

live tonight
xv · signal / status

A state

Dot plus mono uppercase. Six states max per room: live, soon, open, paused, archived, sold. Never a badge swarm.

→ closed list

No new object types per release.

If a room needs a sixteenth, it goes to the system designer. The closed list is what keeps rooms differentiable without proliferation.

Cross-world rules. Every motion respects prefers-reduced-motion (static fallback to end state). Threshold motion plays once; chamber motion is on demand. Object focus uses p-ease-camera at 720ms. Portal hover uses p-ease-tactile at 180ms. Mode transitions (Guided → Wild) cross-fade at 360ms. If a motion needs explanation, it is wrong.

iii · operator

"The system follows."

For internal & advanced users. Fix, migrate, inspect, recover, QA. Hidden by default behind a long-press on the glyph. Never client-facing.

One spectrum, not two toggles. Every room starts Guided. Wild features unlock progressively as the owner reaches for them — never as a "Pro plan" upsell. Operator mode lives behind an internal-only handshake; clients should not know it exists unless something needed to be recovered, and even then the recovery is presented in their language, not ours.

x.

Do not flatten

§ 10 · rulebook

Twelve rules for future designers and engineers. If a release violates one of these, it does not ship.

i.

Rooms are not website templates.

A room is a place, not a layout. If you can describe it as "header · hero · features · CTA · footer," it has flattened. Rebuild from the kit's spatial logic, not from a sitemap.

ii.

Not all rooms should look clean.

Some rooms are loud. Some are loose. Some are torn. The system supports controlled weirdness. If every room renders to the same beige editorial, the system has failed.

iii.

Do not remove weirdness in the name of polish.

A glitch, a marquee, a torn moodboard tile, an asymmetric arrangement — these are the system's signature. Polish without identity is slop.

iv.

Never hide the spatial metaphor.

"Page," "section," "block," "widget" are banned from user-facing copy. Use room, chamber, object, threshold, portal, trace. If engineers need internal vocabulary, fine — the user never sees it.

v.

The cockpit is not a CMS.

No fill-in forms as the primary surface. The owner edits by touching the live room. The inspector is secondary. "Enter your headline" is forbidden.

vi.

Do not invent live social proof.

No fake "27 people viewing." No invented attendees. Traces show what actually happened in the room. Honesty is part of the premium.

vii.

Public rooms never expose editor state.

No selection rings, no hover handles, no "draft" watermarks bleeding into the visitor view. The visitor and the owner see different surfaces. Always.

viii.

Never sacrifice mobile safety for desktop wildness.

However wild a desktop room becomes, a mobile-safe recovery view must exist. CTAs always reachable. Chambers stack cleanly. Commercial function is a baseline, not a tier.

ix.

Do not over-polish away identity.

A room that looks "almost good enough for Apple" is wrong. A room should look like the person whose room it is — even if that person's taste is loud, regional, devotional, or strange.

x.

Do not borrow another world's verbs.

A gallery does not "marquee." A booth does not "breathe." Motion verbs are the world's signature. Mixing them dissolves both.

xi.

"New age" is not astrology-app generic.

No zodiac, no crystals, no purple gradients, no fake spirituality. "New age" means ritualised entry, aura, breath, trace, threshold — atmosphere, not aesthetic cliché.

xii.

If you can ship it from a Figma template, you have failed.

Each pilot is hand-directed. Templates are the floor of the industry; Presence's floor is bbb.vision. Anything less is template work and should not enter the catalogue.

xi.

Implementation handoff

§ 11 · for engineers

Where the design system lives, what each room owns, how to add a new world. Read before designing or building the fifth room.

i · architecture

Where things live

  • assets/colors_and_type.css — single token source. All --p-*, --ck-*, kit themes.
  • pilots/<room>/ — one folder per room. Own CSS, JS, threshold + chamber.
  • pilots/editor.html — cockpit shell. Reads the room as data, never embeds it.
  • direction/ — this pack. Versioned.
ii · adding a world

How to add the fifth room

  • Write the world's spatial logic (atlas card) before any CSS.
  • Declare its motion verbs — six max — and add them to the grammar table.
  • Add a .kit-* class with --rb, --rs, --rt, --rm, --ra, --rg, --rr, --rhw.
  • Reuse object types from the closed list. No new object types per release.
  • Cleared the floor? Add it to the catalogue.
iii · accessibility

Non-negotiables

  • prefers-reduced-motion always honoured; static fallback shows end state.
  • Mobile hit targets ≥ 44px.
  • Public visitor view never exposes editor chrome.
  • Save state every action; never lose work on a refresh.
  • Premium > performance, but never > legibility.
